Showcase Screen Concept: The Drafting Zone
(Fiber Potential Flow Streamlines & Torsional Twist Wave Physics)
________________________________________
The Concept: Your items do not sit in a designed interface. The entire canvas is the drafting zone of a working spinner's cottage at firelight — and it is alive with mathematics. Every thread in existence, from the coarsest warp wool to the finest bone-bobbin lace, comes into being through two physical processes running simultaneously: fiber flowing toward a twist source and torsional twist propagating back along the forming thread. Both of these are rendered as continuous, full-canvas 2D geometry by a CustomPainter running every frame — making this screen a functioning visualisation of the invisible fiber physics that every drop spindle, great wheel, and carding paddle in your collection was built to control.
The canvas has no static background. There is only the hearthside darkness — Hearth Dark (#0D0A07) — and the living fiber field streaming through it.
________________________________________
The Primary 2D Canvas Layer — The Fiber Potential Flow Field:
The defining visual of this screen is a 2D potential flow streamline field painted across the entire canvas by a CustomPainter every frame. Potential flow is the exact mathematical framework textile engineers use to model fiber drafting — the smooth, irrotational drawing of fiber toward a twist source — and here it renders the whole canvas as a top-down fiber dynamics diagram.
Each node contributes to the field through its physical type. Spindle nodes (drop spindles, high-wheel spinners) are modelled as sinks — fiber flows into them as they consume raw roving. Their velocity potential is:
φ_sink(x, y) = −(q_i / 2π) × ln(√((x−xᵢ)² + (y−yᵢ)²))
Distaff nodes are modelled as sources — fiber flows outward from their held raw fiber mass:
φ_source(x, y) = +(q_i / 2π) × ln(r)
Carding paddle nodes are modelled as doublets — a source-sink pair in close proximity — which produces the local circulation cell of a carding action: a closed loop of aligned fiber in their immediate neighbourhood, exactly as two carding paddles passing in opposition create a pocket of ordered, parallel fiber between their wire teeth.
The total velocity field V(x, y) = ∇(Σφᵢ) is traced into streamlines by the CustomPainter using 4th-order Runge-Kutta integration, seeded from evenly-spaced points along the top canvas edge. The streamlines part smoothly around non-spinning nodes (distaffs, skein winders) exactly as flow theory predicts — curving around them in smooth, organic arcs before converging toward spindle sinks. The result is a canvas full of pale ivory flowing curves: fiber streamlines of varying density, dense and bright near active spindle sinks, sparse and dim in the calm mid-field, organic and soft-looking in a way no other physics produces — unmistakably textile in feeling, mathematically exact in origin.
The Secondary 2D Canvas Layer — The Torsional Twist Pattern:
A second CustomPainter renders the torsional twist state of each streamline. At regular arc-length intervals s along every streamline, the painter draws a small oriented ellipse representing the thread's cross-section viewed at that point — its major axis rotated by the local cumulative twist angle:
θ(s) = Σ (τᵢ × Δs)
where the local twist rate τᵢ = ωᵢ / v(s) is the spindle's angular velocity divided by the local fiber velocity from the flow field. Near an active spindle sink where velocity is high and angular velocity intense, the ellipses rotate rapidly along the streamline — tightly-spaced, fast-turning — a dense twist, a fine thread. Far from the spindle where the field is slow, the ellipses rotate gently — widely-spaced, a lazy twist, coarse roving. The combined image of smooth flowing streamlines marked with rotating cross-section ellipses is visually identical to a textile engineering diagram of a spinning operation's drafting zone — the invisible physics of thread formation made permanently visible across the canvas.
________________________________________
The Interaction (The Magic):
•	Spinning spindle nodes rotate continuously — the whorl's simulated angular velocity drives the twist rate τ of their incoming streamlines, and the streamlines visibly spiral inward in a slow logarithmic vortex at the sink point. Tap a spindle node to increase its spin speed — the sink strength q increases, more streamlines converge toward it, the cross-section ellipses along incoming threads tighten from coarse to fine in real time. The haptic delivers a smooth, rising-frequency pulse as the spindle accelerates, exactly like a drop spindle being flicked to spin.
•	Tapping anywhere on the canvas sends a torsional twist pulse outward — a brief perturbation in the twist field rendered as a momentary radial ring where all cross-section ellipses along streamlines within the ring's radius snap to the same orientation simultaneously, then spring back. This is the exact physics of a sudden spin imparted to a forming thread propagating back up the fiber as a torsional wave — rendered as a visible ring of momentary alignment across the canvas. The haptic is a single, clean, soft tap — the flick of a spinner's wrist.
•	Dragging a carding paddle node across the canvas leaves a combing wake — the streamlines immediately behind the paddle temporarily straighten into parallel lines (the ordered, combed fiber), overriding the surrounding flow field curvature for a width matching the paddle's teeth span. The parallel zone slowly dissolves back into the natural flow over 3 seconds as the field re-establishes itself — the fiber relaxing after combing, the ordered alignment gradually yielding to the ambient twist field.
•	Two bone bobbin lace nodes brought within proximity of each other begin to interact through a Lissajous crossing trace — each bobbin traces an elliptical orbit (its natural drift path), and where their orbits cross, the CustomPainter renders the intersection as a small, softly glowing interlacing cross in Bone White — a pillow lace ground stitch, forming automatically from the geometry of two bobbins interacting. As more bobbin nodes cluster together, their crossing traces accumulate into a genuine lace ground pattern: torchon triangles, Honiton oval fillings, naturally emerging from the mathematics of their combined orbital paths. Separate the bobbins and the lace pattern dissolves thread by thread.
•	Long-pressing any node reveals its yarn count — the local flow velocity magnitude |V(xᵢ, yᵢ)| at the node's position, mapped to the traditional worsted count system: Count = (yards per pound) / 560. Displayed as a live readout in IBM Plex Mono: "WORSTED COUNT: 2/32 — FINE WOOL" or "LINEN COUNT: 40/1 — DRESS WEIGHT". The surrounding streamlines brighten momentarily to their full ivory intensity, revealing the full flow geometry in the node's neighbourhood.
•	Skein-winder clock nodes act as field integrators — the total streamline flux passing through their four-arm X-silhouette is computed each frame and accumulated as a running yardage count, displayed as a tiny number at the node's hub, incrementing in real time. When the count reaches the target yardage for that skein type, the click mechanism fires: a single, clean, authoritative haptic click, and the node briefly flares amber.

The Visuals: A palette drawn from the materials of the pioneer-era spinner's hearth: Hearth Dark (#0D0A07), Raw Wool Cream (#F0E8D0), Spindle Amber (#D4920A), Dressed Flax Blue (#8AA8C8), Bone White (#EDE8D8), and Teasel Brown (#6A4A2A). Streamlines render in Raw Wool Cream at varying opacity proportional to local flow velocity — bright ivory near spindle sinks, fading to a barely visible whisper in the calm field. Cross-section twist ellipses render in a slightly warmer ivory, their varying rotation density visually communicating thread quality across the canvas. Nodes are rendered as precise 2D engineering profiles: the drop spindle as a tapered hardwood shaft in Spindle Amber with a flat circular whorl at its base; the high-wheel great spinner as a full front elevation — spokes radiating from the hub, the drive band visible as a curved line looping from wheel rim to spindle arm whorl; the carding paddle as two rectangular paddles in Teasel Brown with rows of fine wire-tooth lines across their faces; the skein-winder clock as a four-armed X-reel with the click mechanism visible at the hub as a small pawl-and-ratchet detail; the bone lace bobbin as a slender turned spool in Bone White with a glass bead weight rendered at its base.
________________________________________
The Focus Transition (The Measure of the Skein):
To inspect an item you must perform the defining act of the pioneer-era spinner: measuring off the skein — counting the exact yardage of spun thread onto the skein-winder clock reel, waiting for the mechanism to click at the precise count that defines a legal, tradeable skein.
A skein-winder clock reel materialises at the canvas centre — rendered as a precise 2D front elevation, four arms in an X, a pawl-and-ratchet click counter at the hub. The fiber flow streamlines across the entire canvas begin to curve slowly inward toward the reel — the whole potential flow field reorienting as the reel becomes the dominant sink — and threads visibly begin winding around its arms in slow, meditative accumulations, each wrap adding to a running yardage counter at the hub displayed in IBM Plex Mono. The twist ellipses along the inward-spiralling threads show the accumulated twist of the entire session's fiber being wound off into the skein.
The target yardage is specific to each item's thread tradition: 240 yards for a hank of dressed linen, 560 yards for a standard worsted wool skein, 120 yards for a bobbin of pillow lace thread. As the counter approaches the target, the haptic engine begins a slow, rhythmic pulse — one beat per wrap. At the exact target count, the click mechanism fires: a single, clean, perfectly weighted haptic click — the sound of a century of spinners hearing the reel tell them their skein was done. The entire flow field freezes, all streamlines crystallising into their final wound positions.
The reel arms splay slightly outward, the skein released. The Focus Panel unfurls like a length of woven cloth slowly unrolling from the reel — the panel's surface rendered with a visible warp-and-weft texture, pale cream threads crossing in a fine grid — the item's details appearing as if stitched in fine cross-stitch lettering: tool material and wood species, fiber type for which designed, geographic craft tradition (Welsh, Colonial American, Scandinavian, Flemish), maker or cottage-industry provenance, era, and the photograph printed as if pressed between the pages of a Victorian household pattern book — warm, soft, slightly foxed at the edges, the tool resting on a bed of raw unprocessed fleece.
